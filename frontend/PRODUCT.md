# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Qurate is for naive or non-technical users who need the benefits of a database without first learning SQL. They may be founders, operators, analysts, students, small teams, or business users who have data in a database, CSV, or Excel file and want to query, manage, summarize, or visualize it using natural language and voice.

Users are often trying to move from a raw data source to an answer quickly: asking questions, creating or inspecting tables, generating charts, importing spreadsheets as databases, and using multilingual voice prompts when typing or SQL syntax would slow them down.

## Product Purpose

Qurate makes database work accessible through conversational AI. It lets users talk to their data in plain language, including voice input across multiple languages, and receive useful responses as text, tables, or graphs.

The product exists to remove the dependency on SQL knowledge for common database tasks. Success means a user can import an Excel or CSV file, form a usable database, ask questions in their own words, and trust the resulting tables, charts, and actions without feeling intimidated by technical database tooling.

## Brand Personality

Simple, trustworthy, empowering.

Qurate should feel like a capable assistant that lowers the barrier to data work. The tone should be clear and reassuring, with enough intelligence to feel powerful but enough restraint to avoid overwhelming first-time or non-technical users.

## Anti-references

Qurate must not feel like intimidating enterprise software. Avoid dense admin-tool complexity, jargon-heavy database interfaces, developer-first SQL workbenches, and layouts that make basic actions feel risky or technical.

The product should also avoid AI hype that distracts from the real workflow. Voice, multilingual prompting, spreadsheet import, database management, and insight generation should feel practical and usable, not decorative.

## Design Principles

1. Make the first useful query obvious.
2. Keep database power visible, but never intimidating.
3. Treat voice and multilingual input as first-class workflows.
4. Build trust through clear states, previews, confirmations, and readable results.
5. Let imported spreadsheets become usable databases with minimal ceremony.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Prioritize multilingual and voice-first inclusivity. The interface should support users who are more comfortable speaking than typing, and users who prefer regional or non-English languages for prompting.

Design decisions should preserve clarity for first-time users: readable labels, direct feedback, obvious controls, and interaction patterns that do not require prior database expertise.
